Our Story

Built from inside
the fence line.

Equine Data Verse started with a question that wouldn't go away: why does a sport this extraordinary have almost no data? Not accessible data. Not data at the level where riders can actually use it.

After 200+ hours of competition footage from events across the globe, the patterns became undeniable. The difference between a 9 and a 10 is measurable. The gap between what elite riders receive and what everyone else gets is fixable. The volunteer problem has a technology answer.

We're a small team in New Zealand building something we believe has never been built quite this way before — tools that are genuinely affordable, genuinely useful, and completely agnostic about whether you're chasing Olympic selection or shitting yourself at 65cm.

Why we exist

The sport we love has real problems.

Three things that have stayed the same for too long — and the reason Equine Data Verse exists.

01
Volunteer dependency

Hundreds of generous people sit out in all weathers to make events happen. Technology exists right now that could free them up to actually enjoy the sport they give so much to.

02
A sport hard to follow

Eventing is extraordinary. But for a spectator who doesn't already know it, it's nearly impossible to understand what's happening. That costs the sport its next generation of fans.

03
Feedback only for the few

Elite riders have coaches with slow-motion cameras and data. The 65cm rider gets a 30-second debrief if they're lucky. Every rider at every level deserves tools that help them improve.

Two clears. Same fence. Completely different jumps.
Both horses jump the corner fence clean — no faults, same score on the card. But watch the footage. One horse meets it perfectly: balanced approach, clean bascule, lands in rhythm. The other drifts left, chips in a short stride, scrambles through and lands on the forehand. Same result. Completely different jump. The scorecard captures none of this — yet one of those horses is being trained toward a habit that will cost them eventually.

Scenario · Volunteers
Could technology genuinely fix eventing's volunteer crisis?
The average fence judge at a cross-country event stands in a field for 4–6 hours. They record a fence number and a fault code on paper. They don't see most of the course. They miss the dressage, the showjumping, and usually the podium. Many events are now cancelled or run at reduced capacity because they can't get enough volunteers — not because of demand, but because of attrition.

What was the most useful feedback you ever got after a competition?
Most competition feedback is either too vague to act on ("nice round!"), too brief to be useful (a 10-second conversation after the horse float is packed), or simply never happens.
